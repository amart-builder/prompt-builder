If You Remember One Thing

Talk to it like a brilliant new hire on day one.

Claude is sharp, fast, and capable, but on your request it knows nothing about your deals, your clients, or the way you like things done. It can't read your mind. Everything that's obvious to you, like who's going to read this, what "good" looks like, and how long it should be, has to be said out loud.

The test: if you handed your request to a smart new analyst who'd never seen your business, would they know exactly what to do?

If the answer is no, Claude won't either. The whole craft of prompting is just closing that gap by telling it what the new hire would need to know.

The Structure

Every good prompt has the same skeleton.

Four parts, top to bottom. Fill them in and you've got a strong prompt before you've even thought about technique.

1
Goal
What you want, and the format you want it back in.
2
Context
The why, the situation, and who it's for.
3
Material
Any document or example you're working from.
4
The Ask
Your actual question or instruction, placed last.
Working from a document?

Don't retype it. Drag the file straight into the chat box, or use the attach button, then ask your question underneath. Claude reads the whole thing. (If you'd rather paste the text in, paste the document first and put your question at the end.) Perfect for a CIM, a contract, or a financials packet.

The Craft

Five habits that make each part land.

Those four parts are the skeleton. These five habits are what separate a weak version of each from a strong one.

1

Be specific.

Spell out the format, the length, and the scope. Vague in, vague out.

Instead of "Summarize this CIM." → "Summarize this CIM as a one-page brief, five bullets, skimmable in 60 seconds."
2

Always give the why.

The reason is what lets Claude make a hundred small judgment calls the way you would. It's the difference between a generic answer and yours.

Instead of "Keep it short." → "Keep it short. It's for a buyer who gets fifty of these a week and reads on his phone."
3

Show an example of "good."

Got a teaser or an email you loved? Paste it in and say "match this style." One good example beats a paragraph of description.

Try "Here's a teaser that landed well last quarter. Write the new one in the same voice and structure." → paste the old one
4

Tell it to ask.

The single best line for avoiding confident, wrong answers. End your request with it.

Add "If anything's unclear or you're missing information you need, ask me instead of guessing."
5

It takes you literally.

Opus does exactly what you say. Tell it to act, not advise, and name the scope so it doesn't stop halfway.

Instead of "Can you suggest some edits?" → "Rewrite the second paragraph to be warmer, and check every section, not just the first."
See It In Action

Same task. Two very different results.

Say you're re-engaging a practice owner who went quiet after a first call. Here's the lazy version, and the version that gets a usable email on the first try.

✗ The lazy version
"Write an email to Dr. Sarah Chen about selling her practice."
✓ The version that works

"I'm following up with Dr. Sarah Chen, who owns a physical therapy practice in Denver. We had a great first call two weeks ago about a potential sale, and then she went quiet. I want to gently re-engage her, with no pressure.

What matters: she's emotional about selling. She built this practice over twenty years. So the tone needs to be warm and respectful, never salesy. We do have two other interested buyers, which I'd like to mention as a soft nudge, but it can't feel like a threat.

Write a short email, four to six sentences, no subject line. Human and friendly. If you need any details from me to make it land, ask before writing."

✓What & who: re-engage Dr. Chen after she went quiet
✓The why: she's emotional; she built it over twenty years
✓Tone & audience: warm, respectful, never salesy
✓Format: four to six sentences, no subject line
✓The out: ask if you're missing anything

The second one took thirty extra seconds to write. It saves you three rounds of "no, not like that."

Skip These

Tricks that don't actually work.

You may have heard "hacks" for getting more out of AI. Most do nothing on a model this capable, and a few make it worse. The research is clear; don't bother with these:

✗

"You are a world-class expert..."

Telling Claude to pretend it's an expert doesn't make it more accurate. (It can help set the tone, but not the correctness.)

✗

Bribes and threats.

"I'll tip you $200," "this is CRITICAL," "or I'll get fired." Controlled studies show zero benefit. Claude doesn't respond to pressure.

✗

"Think step by step."

Older advice from an earlier era of AI. Today's Claude already reasons on its own, so adding this mostly just slows it down.

✗

ALL CAPS and !!!.

Over-the-top urgency tends to backfire and make the output worse.

What actually works is refreshingly boring: be clear, give context, name the format. That's the whole game.

The Shortcut

Or, don't remember any of this.

Here's the secret: you don't have to do any of this by hand. We've set up a tool that does it for you.

Meet /prompt

When you have something that really matters (an important email, a document to digest, anything where you want a genuinely great result), type /prompt and then brain-dump everything in your head. Messy is fine. Half-sentences are fine.

It reads your dump, fills in everything above for you, and writes a strong, complete prompt. Then it shows you that prompt and waits for your go-ahead before running anything, so you stay in control. Once you say go, it runs the prompt and double-checks the result against what you actually meant before showing you.

You type
/prompt ugh i need to email the practice owner who went quiet after our call, keep it warm, remind her we've got two other buyers interested but don't sound pushy
↓
It shows you the prompt
It turns your dump into a clean, complete prompt and asks, "Run this, or want to tweak it first?" Nothing happens until you say go.
↓
You get back
A finished email that's warm, mentions the other buyers as a soft nudge, and doesn't sound desperate. Checked before you ever see it.
When to use it: not for quick things ("what's 15% of $4.2M?"). Use it when the output matters and you want it to be excellent.
Get Set Up

Two minutes, one time.

Install the /prompt tool once and it's there whenever you need it.

1

Open a new chat in Claude.

2

Paste in the setup instructions below and send. Claude will install it for you.

Install a custom skill called "prompt" from GitHub:

1. Clone https://github.com/amart-builder/prompt-builder into a temp folder.
2. Create the directory ~/.claude/skills/ if it doesn't exist.
3. Copy the repo's prompt/ folder to ~/.claude/skills/prompt
   (so the files end up at ~/.claude/skills/prompt/SKILL.md and
   ~/.claude/skills/prompt/reference/prompt-engineering.md).
4. Confirm both files are in place and tell me the skill is installed.
3

When it says it's done, start a fresh chat. Type / and you'll see prompt in the list.

A restart is what makes the new tool show up, just like closing and reopening an app after an update.

4

From then on: /prompt + brain dump, whenever it counts.
